    Background Information


    Flaviviruses are small, enveloped RNA viruses that contain pathogens that can cause several diseases in humans. The flavivirus encodes 11 proteins within the RNA with 3 structural proteins and 8 non-structural proteins (the replication complex). This replication complex is what we focused on in our research. Specifically, NS3 section (the helicase and protease) and NS5 section (methyltransferase and polymerase). Those protein structures are what replicates the RNA.


    Proteins envelope the outside of the virus. Flaviviruses are spread through arthropod vectors like mosquitos and ticks. The mosquitos and ticks infect mammals (including humans) and birds. To counter flaviviruses, antibodies that prevent the virus from attacking the cell are administered to an infected person. The antibodies do this by binding to the virus and therefore blocking all the receptors needed to bind to healthy cells. Once the receptors are fully blocked with antibodies, the virus will bind to an immune cell and become degraded and harmless. After that, if the same person is infected with the same virus, the antibodies can be made, and the cycle will repeat.

    The problem with this process is that there are several variations of the same virus. If someone is immune to one version but not another, then being infected with another version can result in antibody dependent enhancement. This is when the presence of an antibody enhances the replication of a virus instead of stopping it. This is because the wrong antibodies will bind to a virus which leaves healthy cells and immune cells susceptible to the virus to bind to them.

    Protein structures are important in this research because the structure of the protein determines the function of the protein. We used AlphaFold to determine protein structure. AlphaFold is an AI created by DeepMind that predicts the structure of protein's alpha-helixes, Beta-strands, and coils with high accuracy.

    • Day 1

    On the first day, my group needed to pick a flavivirus, and my pair needed to choose a replication section (NS3 or NS5) to focus on. In our research, we focused on Zika. My partner and I chose to study the NS5 section. If an antiviral binds to the NS5 structure, it can prevent the replication of the flavivirus. The next thing we needed to do was determine an evolutionary timeline for the flavivirus. For this, we did multiple sequence alignment. We did this using JalView. Looking at a base evolutionary tree (provided by the research instructor), we grabbed our virus (Zika) and 11 more viruses closely related to it. We got the protein sequences for the NS5 structure from a protein sequence database (provided by the research instructor) and then aligned the sequences of all 12 viruses to find the area where all the proteins matched up (fully conserved site). When a fully (or mostly) conserved site is found, a common ancestor of the virus can be assumed.


    • Day 2

    On the second day, we focused on phylogenetics. Phylogenetics is how different species are related to each other in terms of evolution. We learned tree topology nomenclature (tree roots, internal nodes, tips, etc.) and how to use these elements to form a proportional topology tree. We also learned that the numbers (SH-like) on the nodes of the tree are used to represent confidence in the generated tree. If a node has an SH-like of 85>, the tree has high accuracy confidence. If the SH-like is 75<, the tree has low accuracy confidence. To make the tree, we put our protein sequences (from day 1) into a software called Fig-Tree which generated the most likely evolutionary tree (based off the internal node confidence). After that, we transferred the generated tree to another software called IQ-Tree which changed the structure of the tree from a circular shape to a traditional evolutionary tree shape. This software also allowed us to alter the length of the branches to make the tree readable and accurate in terms of proportions.

    • Day 3

    Before day three started, the research instructor used PockDrug to find pockets in our chosen protein (NS5) that can bind to small drug-like molecules. The measure of the ability for the pocket to bind to a drug is called druggability. Druggability is measured numerically from 0-1, where 1 has the highest probability of binding to a drug. My partner and I decided to focus on pocket 18 and pocket 25 of the Zika NS5 protein structure. Using an SQ-Lite database, we were able to find the amino acids and positions of our chosen pockets (18 and 25) on the protein structure rendered by AlphaFold. Then, with the protein sequences on JalView (from day 1), we are able to find the conservation scores for both pockets (how much the protein sequences align for all 12 viruses).

    This is where the NS5 and NS3 groups combined for the project. We inputted our conservation scores into a script on Spyder that runs the Shapiro-Wilks test on the data. Spyder is an integrated development environment (IDE) for the Python programming language. The Shapiro-Wilks test determines if the data collected is normal. Our data did not return as normal, so we ran it through a second script to run a Mann-Whitney test. The Mann-Whitney test compares the pockets to determine which ones are more or less conserved.

    The photo shows a p-value yield. The p-value is a comparison to the null hypothesis. The null hypothesis for this research is that there is no difference between the two samples being compared. The Alpha we were given was 0.05. This means that if the p-value is less than the Alpha, then the null hypothesis is rejected and there is a significant difference between the pockets being compared. According to the photo above, the first three comparisons are well below the Alpha value, and therefore the null hypothesis is rejected. However, for the last comparison, the p-value is way above the Alpha value. This means the null hypothesis is valid. Spyder then generated a box plot to organize the site conservation for each pocket. It also generated a bar chart to measure the druggability of each pocket.

    • Day 4

    On day four, we began to put our poster board together. First, we aligned our IQ-Tree evolution tree and JalView protein sequences so each virus on the tree can match their protein sequence for each pocket.

    Once that was done, we opened our AlphaFold model in Pymol. Pymol is a software that allows the user to visually navigate the 3D structures created by AlphaFold. On Pymol, we inserted the positions determined by SQ-Lite on day 3. These positions marked where the pockets are found on our respective protein structure (Zika NS5). Once the positions are entered, they are marked by purple squares on the protein render. We then changed the squares to spheres on each protein so they can be easily visible on the presentation board.

    The colors of these renders are important. The dark blue means the prediction is highly confident, the light blue means the prediction is fairly confident, the yellow means the prediction is less confident, and the orange means the prediction is not confident. Pocket 18 is mostly dark blue but has some areas of light blue and yellow. The prediction for this being the protein structure of Zika NS5 is overall fairly confident. However, pocket 25 is entirely dark blue, which means the prediction for this being the structure of the protein is very high. This is particularly important because (as we learned in the background information) the protein structure determines the function of the protein. If the algorithm is not confident with the structure that is generated, we cannot confidently determine the function of the protein. We then added the two charts Spyder created, the AlphaFold model and the IQ-Tree/JalView evolution tree protein sequence to our board. We added a title and introduction section as well to the board.

    • Day 5

    On day five, we focused on drawing conclusions so that we could present the board we created at a small symposium. We found that the Zika NS5 pocket 25 had the highest conservation score and druggability of the four protein sections we were researching. Along with that, pocket 25 also had the highest confidence in the rendered AlphaFold structure. This means that of any pocket (of the four we studied) pocket 25 in the Zika NS5 protein should be the one of main focus for finding an antiviral to bind to. This is because with high druggability, the antiviral is most likely to bind to this section of the protein, which means it can effectively prevent the replication of the flavivirus RNA. With a high conservation score, the antiviral is likely to work against several variations of Zika and other flaviviruses. We found that NS3 pocket 21 had the lowest conservation score and that NS5 pocket 18 had the lowest druggability score. As a group, we determined that a limitation of this study was that we could only compare 12 viruses to each other. This is quite small in the context of the many flaviviruses out there. To address this limitation, we decided that a future direction to take this experiment in is to make the scale larger. Compare many more viruses so the final determined pocket can be more general and not restricted to only 12 viruses.

    At the symposium, my group of four split into pairs again. This time I was partnered with a person from the NS3 section pair. The symposium board was visited by members of a different research project, professors that specialize in similar fields to what we studied, and our own members of the Bio3 Spring Forward Workshop. For half of the symposium, my pair was directed to present our research findings, explain the steps we took to complete our project and to answer any questions at the end. During the other half of the symposium, my pair was to swap places with the other pair in my group. We were then instructed to walk around and listen to other's presentations and ask questions of our own.
